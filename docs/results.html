3  Results

3.1 Sample characteristics and descriptive statistics

We analyzed Gulf water temperatures measured at the Dauphin Island, AL buoy and hourly electricity demand from the regional grid operator. The water-temperature record includes 8,841 half-hour observations from January 01, 2025 through December 31, 2025. The demand dataset contributes 4,344 hourly observations from January 01, 2025 through July 01, 2025.

Data cleaning steps are summarized below:

Water temperature records were cleaned of sentinel missing values, range-checked against physically plausible Gulf bounds, converted to °F, and aggregated to hourly means (rationale and conventions are documented in Appendix A).

  • Electricity demand: limited to observed hourly demand (demand_mw), with a peak-demand indicator defined as the top 10% of demand hours.
  • Merged analysis file: constructed by joining hourly Gulf temperatures to hourly demand on local timestamp.

3.1.1 Descriptive statistics: water temperature

On average, Gulf water temperature during the 2025 observation window was 72.1°F (SD 12.7°F), with a median of 73.2°F and a maximum of 92.5°F. High-temperature conditions relevant for plant operations were not rare: 20% of half‑hour readings exceeded the 85°F design assumption.

Monthly summaries (Table 3.1) show that exceedances of 85°F are concentrated in the late-summer and early‑fall months, when both the 95th percentile and maximum temperatures peak. This seasonality is important for planning maintenance and contingency capacity.

3.1.2 Descriptive statistics: electricity demand and peak periods

Hourly demand over the same period averaged 2546 MW (SD 850 MW), with a typical hour (median) at 2276 MW and a maximum of 5758 MW. By construction, approximately 10% of hours are classified as peak‑demand hours.

Demand exhibits the expected intra‑annual pattern, with higher loads during hot months and lower loads during shoulder seasons. Because both water temperature and demand peak in warm months, there is potential for coincident operational stress.

3.1.3 Time-series overview

The time series (Figure 3.1) confirms a pronounced warm‑season hump, with extended stretches where hourly average water temperature exceeds 85°F. These stretches translate directly into periods when the plant would be forced to operate at reduced capacity under current design constraints.

3.2 Primary analysis: probability and frequency of exceeding 85°F

3.2.1 Overall probability of exceedance

At the hourly level (after aggregating 30‑minute readings), the plant would have faced reduced‑capacity conditions in 11% of observed hours. Scaling this observed proportion to a full year implies that, under 2025‑like conditions, the plant should expect approximately 935 hours (about 39 days) per year above 85°F. Over a 5‑year planning horizon, this corresponds to roughly 4677 hours (about 194.9 days) of required reduced‑capacity operation, assuming no further warming trend beyond 2025.

Monthly probabilities highlight that exceedances are highly seasonal, with very low risk in winter and spring and substantial risk concentrated in the hottest months. Operationally, this means that reduced‑capacity events are not evenly scattered throughout the year but clustered into specific high‑risk seasons.

3.2.2 Crude and adjusted models of exceedance risk

We fit binomial logistic regression models for the binary outcome “hourly water temperature above 85°F” (Agresti 2013).

  • Crude model: intercept‑only, overall exceedance prevalence on the logit scale.
  • Peak‑only model: adds an indicator for peak‑demand hours (top 10% of load).
  • Adjusted model: adds month indicators so that the peak‑load contrast is estimated within calendar month, reducing spurious association driven purely by shared summer seasonality between temperature and air‑conditioning load.

The intercept‑only specification reproduces the marginal exceedance frequency. For the peak‑demand term, interpret the odds ratio as a multiplicative change in the odds of exceedance relative to non‑peak hours; when exceedance is not too rare, odds ratios approximate relative risk directionally for prioritization. Practical conclusions rest on whether confidence intervals for the peak‑demand odds ratio exclude 1 at the upper tail (elevated odds during peak) and on the magnitude; large multipliers matter more than p-values for sparse hourly events.

The month‑adjusted model asks a narrower question: conditional on month, are peak‑load hours still more likely to coincide with exceedance? Affirmative evidence supports load‑related concentration beyond seasonal co‑movement alone.

The very large odds ratio and wide confidence interval on peak_demandTRUE in the month‑adjusted model reflect (quasi-)complete separation in the observed data. In plain terms, within warm-season months in this sample, nearly every peak-demand hour also exceeded 85°F, which inflates the point estimate but does not change the direction of the effect (higher odds during peak hours).

3.3 Coincidence of high temperatures with high‑demand periods

3.3.1 Cross‑tabulation of exceedance and peak demand

Across the full observation window, the water temperature exceeded 85°F in 427 of the 3999 hours with both temperature and demand data. Of those exceedance hours, 45 occurred during peak‑demand hours. The resulting annualized estimate is approximately 99 hours (about 4.1 days) per year when both Gulf water exceeds 85°F and electricity demand is in the top 10%.

Note that the demand series ends July 1, so this coincidence calculation excludes July, August, and September entirely, the three months when Table 3.1 shows exceedance rates of about 85% (Jul), 78% (Aug), and 25% (Sep). As a result, the annualized coincident-hours figure above should be treated as a lower bound, not a standalone planning input. For rough intuition, if June, the only full summer month with demand data, with 56% exceedance in Table 3.1, is at all representative of the missing July–September window, then true coincident hours could plausibly be on the order of 3–5× the reported estimate.

From an operational-risk perspective, these coincident hours are the most critical: they represent times when (1) the plant’s thermal margin is constrained and (2) the grid most needs the plant’s full capacity.

3.3.2 Joint distribution visualization

The scatter (Figure 3.2) shows that many of the hottest water‑temperature hours occur at moderate‑to‑high demand levels, with a notable cluster in the upper‑right quadrant where both temperature and demand are high. This pattern is consistent with elevated odds of exceedance during peak‑demand hours in Table 3.4.

3.4 Secondary analyses

3.4.1 Seasonal patterns and timing within the year

The smoothed seasonal curve (Figure 3.3) shows a distinct high‑risk window when temperatures move above 85°F and remain elevated for several weeks.

3.4.2 Interaction between season and peak demand

Seasonal subgroup analyses (Table 3.6) show that exceedances are almost entirely a summer and early‑fall phenomenon, with negligible risk in winter and spring. Within summer, exceedance during peak‑demand hours can exceed non‑peak hours, consistent with effect modification by both season and load level.

3.4.3 Sensitivity to peak‑demand definition

Alternative top‑quantile cutoffs (for example, 5% versus 15%) change the count of peak hours but rarely overturn the qualitative pattern: a non‑zero set of hours exhibits both high temperature and high relative load. Re‑estimation under alternative rules is straightforward given the merged hourly file.
