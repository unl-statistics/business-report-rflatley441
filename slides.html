Background

The problem

Gulf Coast power plants use nearby ocean water as a heat sink it absorbs waste heat so the plant can keep running at full capacity.

When that water gets too warm, the plant cannot transfer heat fast enough and must reduce output to avoid overheating.

When these plants were built in the 1970s, engineers assumed Gulf water would stay below 85°F. That assumption is now regularly broken.

The dangerous overlap

Gulf water peaks in temperature during late summer the same season when electricity demand is highest due to air conditioning. The plant is constrained exactly when the grid needs it most.

Design assumption (1970)
85°F

Maximum Gulf water temperature the cooling system was built to handle.

What we observed in 2025
92.5°F

Maximum recorded water temperature at Dauphin Island, AL.

Research Question

Research question

How often does observed Gulf water temperature exceed the 85°F threshold, and how often do those exceedances coincide with peak electricity demand?

This is an operational question not a long-run climate study. Over a near-term planning horizon, how much of a constraint is warm Gulf water, and does it bind at the worst possible moments?

Two things I measured
Exceedance

Did water temp exceed 85°F?

Yes or no for each hour. When yes, the plant is thermally constrained.

Peak demand

Was demand in the top 10%?

Yes or no for each hour. When yes, the grid most needs the plant at full output.

The critical hours are when both answers are yes at the same time.

Data & Methods

How I combined temperature
and demand data

Dataset 1 Water Temperature

NOAA NDBC · Dauphin Island, AL

30-minute sea surface temperature readings for all of 2025. Cleaned, converted °C → °F, and aggregated to hourly means.

Key variable: Did the hourly temperature exceed 85°F? (yes / no)

Dataset 2 Electricity Demand

U.S. EIA-930 · Southern Balancing Authority

Hourly demand in MW, January–July 2025. Time-aligned to match the temperature data.

Key variable: Was demand in the top 10% of load hours? (peak / non-peak)

Method

Logistic Regression

Three models: baseline prevalence, peak-load effect, and month-adjusted to remove seasonal confounding.

Results reported as odds ratios with 95% confidence intervals, translated to expected hours and days per year.

Important caveat

These figures are meant to inform planning decisions. They assume near-term conditions mirror 2025. NOAA reports Gulf sea surface temperatures rose ~1°C between 1970 and 2020 meaning the true risk is likely higher than these figures suggest.

Results

The problem happens at the
worst possible time

Red points are hours when Gulf water exceeded 85°F and the plant had to reduce output. They appear across the full range of demand levels — including the highest.

~99 hours per year when water exceeded 85°F and demand was in the top 10% simultaneously — and that count excludes July, August, and September entirely.

Recommendations

Three actionable
planning steps

1

Capacity and Reserves

Incorporate warm-season derate exposure into resource adequacy assessments. Treat annualized coincident hours as a lower bound demand data excludes the three hottest months.

2

Maintenance Scheduling

Shift discretionary work toward shoulder months (October–April) where exceedance risk is near zero. Avoid planned outages in June–September, especially during peak-demand hours.

3

Mitigation Portfolio

Evaluate cooling upgrades, thermal buffering, and demand-side options using a cost–risk screen tied to restricted-output hours and peak overlap the quantities this report estimates directly.

Limitations & Future Work

What the analysis
does not claim

Missing months

The coincidence count is too low

Demand data only run through July 1. July, August, and September the three hottest months are missing from the overlap calculation. Every coincident-hour figure understates the true exposure.

Warming trend

2025 may already be optimistic

The frequency projections assume next year looks like 2025. But Gulf water has warmed ~1°C since 1970. The 85°F threshold will be breached more frequently as Gulf water continues to warm.

Thresholds are assumptions

85°F and "top 10%" are starting points

These numbers are reasonable defaults, but the actual constraint temperature and peak definition should be validated against the specific plant and market context.

Sensor gaps

Exceedance rates may be understated

About 2.6% of temperature readings were flagged and removed. If the sensor tended to fail during the hottest conditions, the reported exceedance rates are slightly too low.

Conclusion

The risk is real
and it is growing

Gulf water temperatures regularly exceed the 85°F design limit and they do so most often during the hours when the grid needs full output. The numbers in this report are lower bounds: the true risk is at least this large, and likely larger.

~935 hrs/yr above 85°F under 2025 conditions
~99 hrs/yr coincident with peak demand (lower bound)
100% of summer peak-demand hours exceeded 85°F
